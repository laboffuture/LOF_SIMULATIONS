    Why phases?

    The Sun always lights half the Moon. As the Moon circles Earth (≈29.5 days New→New), we view that lit half from a changing angle — a sliver, a quarter, the full face, and back.

    Why the same face, always?

    The Moon spins exactly once per orbit — tidal locking. That's why the red beacon never leaves the disc. Untick tidal lock above: with its spin frozen, you'd see every side in a single month.

    Why are eclipses rare?

    The Moon's orbit is tilted 5.1°. A New or Full Moon usually passes just above or below perfect alignment. Only near the nodes — about two seasons a year — do the three bodies line up for an eclipse.

    Tidally locked pairs — a field guide

    Our Moon isn't special: gravity gradually locks most large moons to their planets. Every red beacon below stays aimed at its partner. One famous world only looks locked — spot it.
